The Family Policing Machine: A Data Story
Narrative Structure & Visualization Guide for Bronx Defenders x PiTech

Overall Story Arc
The website should function as a guided descent — the reader enters thinking they're learning about "child protection" and exits understanding they've witnessed a surveillance and punishment system disproportionately aimed at Black and Latino families in poverty. Each section should feel like a door that closes behind you.
The emotional arc: confusion → recognition → outrage → call to action.

Opening: The Hook
Narrative: Every year in New York City, a phone call sets a machine in motion. That call — sometimes anonymous, sometimes retaliatory, sometimes well-intentioned — launches a process that can end with children taken from their parents, families destroyed, and communities surveilled. This is not the child protection system. This is family policing. And in the Bronx, it is inescapable.
Visual: An animated, full-screen counter showing the number of SCR intakes in 2023 (95,590) ticking upward like a clock. Below it, a second counter showing how many were ultimately substantiated (22,120 — 23.1%). Let the gap speak for itself before any text explains it. This sets the stakes immediately and makes the reader feel the scale viscerally.

Act 1: The Phone Call — SCR Intake
Narrative: It begins with a call. New York's Statewide Central Register (SCR) accepts 75% of all calls alleging child maltreatment and routes them to local agencies for investigation — compared to the 50% national average. This means that before a single investigator knocks on a door, New York has already decided that three out of four accusations are worth pursuing. There is no standardized screening tool. The state has never formally examined its own screen-out data.
One in 24 cases originates from an anonymous tip. Only 6.7% of those anonymous tips are ever substantiated — compared to 22.5% for all cases. Yet the family on the other end of that anonymous call will receive an investigator at their door regardless.
Despite years of reform rhetoric, the acceptance rate has barely moved.
Visuals:
Bar chart comparison: NY's 75% acceptance rate vs. the 50% national average. Simple, stark, single comparison. Make the bar for NY dramatically taller. Label it plainly: "New York passes 3 in 4 calls. Most states pass 1 in 2."
Split stat callout: Anonymous tips — 1 in 24 cases, 6.7% substantiated vs. 22.5% for all cases. Display this as two side-by-side large-number callouts with brief labels. The contrast does the work.
Line chart: ACS case volume over the last 20 years, with the COVID dip (2020-21) and subsequent rise clearly annotated. Annotate the COVID period with a pointed, uncomfortable observation: "Cases fell when children were home and out of sight of mandated reporters. They rose again when schools reopened." This reframes what "safety" means in this system. Also annotate the CARES growth from 4% to 22% of cases routed, showing that reform has been incremental and structural problems at the SCR remain unchanged.

Act 2: The Knock at the Door — Investigation
Narrative: An ACS investigator arrives. They do not need a warrant. In fact, between 2010 and 2020, ACS obtained an average of only 94 entry orders per year — out of approximately 56,400 cases annually. That is a warrant rate of less than 0.2%.
Instead, investigators gain entry through coercion. Documented tactics include 3 a.m. visits, threats of immediate removal, and creating public scenes in hallways to shame parents into opening their doors. Some caseworkers frame seeking a court order as "a warrant being put out on you." Inside, investigators rifle through bedrooms, open medicine cabinets, inspect refrigerators, and strip-search children.
In 2021, 44% of Black children and 43% of Latino children in New York City experienced an ACS investigation. 19% of white children did. In 2023, 9.9% of reports against Black parents resulted in emergency removal — more than double the 4.4% rate for white parents.
A Black child in New York City has nearly a 50% chance of being investigated by age 18.
The psychological toll on children is severe and documented. Children blame themselves. A young girl, asked to write a story about the home visits, wrote: "I am a bad kid." A six-year-old, after being asked by an investigator whether she was "suicidal," began repeating the word during every subsequent visit — associating the presence of the state with self-harm.
Yet the majority of these cases lead nowhere. In FY 2023, 56.6% of all intakes were unsubstantiated. The system is a high-volume, low-yield dragnet.
Visuals:
The Warrant Box (inspired by ProPublica): A large rectangle representing all ACS cases (~56,400). Inside it, a tiny box — almost invisible — representing the 94 warrants obtained. No additional explanation needed. Label the big box "ACS Cases" and the tiny box "Cases with a Warrant." Let the visual proportion create the outrage.
Stacked bar or proportional area chart — Race and Investigation Rates: Show the proportion of Black, Latino, and white children investigated in 2021 (44%, 43%, 19%). Use a format where each bar represents "all children of that group" and the shaded portion represents "those investigated." The visual should make it immediately apparent that being Black or Latino in NYC means nearly a coin-flip chance of an ACS investigation by adulthood.
FY 2023 Intake Table — Redesigned as a visual: Take the race/ethnicity breakdown table and render it as a proportional flow or stacked bar showing SCR Intakes → Indicated → Unsubstantiated for each racial group. The sheer volume of unsubstantiated cases against Black and Latino families, compared to the indicated rate, tells the dragnet story visually. Use color to distinguish "indicated" from "unsubstantiated" — the latter should dominate.
Pull quote cards: Intersperse the documented coercive tactics as large, styled pull quotes — "I'm not going to stop coming." / "Why not, if you don't have anything to hide?" These should feel uncomfortable to read. Format them to look like they are being spoken directly at the reader.

Act 3: The Cost of "Help" — Foster Care
Narrative:
When removal happens, the state pays. In 2024, ACS maintained an average of 6,400 children in foster care at a cost of $685,963,000 — roughly $107,200 per child per year.
Consider "Eline" in the Bronx. Her children were removed for three years because her apartment had a rat infestation that made safe food storage impossible. The city paid a foster family more than $1,000 per month to care for her children. The city refused to provide Eline with the funds to secure rat-free housing or a visiting nurse. A $300 monthly housing subsidy could have kept her family together. The state chose the $107,200 option instead.
This is not an anomaly. It is the system's default logic: pay to separate, refuse to stabilize.But what does the state's $107,200 actually buy?
The children who pass through foster care do not emerge unscathed. An estimated 28% of the homeless population spent time in foster care during their youth. Nearly 20% of prison inmates under 30 — and 25% of those with prior convictions — report a foster care history. Girls placed in foster care have a teen birth rate roughly twice that of girls who remained home. Employment in young adulthood is reduced by 11 to 15 percentage points for those placed on the margins of the system. As foster youth age out in New York City, they show extremely high utilization of family and single-adult homeless shelters, and are disproportionate users of inpatient psychiatric care and psychiatric emergency services.
These are not the outcomes of a system that protects children. They are the outcomes of a system that trades one form of harm for another — and in many cases, manufactures harm where it did not previously exist.
The most damning evidence concerns what researchers call "marginal cases" — families where the evidence of harm is ambiguous, where a caseworker could plausibly decide either way. These are precisely the cases most likely to be produced by the dragnet described in Acts 1 and 2: the anonymous tips with a 6.7% substantiation rate, the poverty-coded neglect allegations, the 56.6% of intakes that are never substantiated but proceed anyway. For these families, research on the causal effects of placement finds that children left at home have lower adult arrest, conviction, and imprisonment rates than those removed. For marginal cases, foster care placement can result in an arrest or delinquency rate up to three times higher than if the child had simply stayed home.
The system is not removing the children who most need protection. It is removing the children least likely to need it — and doing them the most measurable harm in the process.
Some researchers argue these poor outcomes reflect selection bias: the children who enter foster care have already experienced severe maltreatment, poverty, and family trauma before the state ever arrives. This is true and worth acknowledging. But it does not explain away the marginal case findings, where researchers specifically control for pre-existing conditions and still find that placement itself causes harm. And even within the system, outcomes are not uniform: children placed in residential facilities or non-family settings consistently experience greater developmental adversity than those placed in family foster care — meaning the system not only removes children unnecessarily, but frequently fails to place them in the least harmful setting when it does. The number of children in foster care has fallen dramatically — from 40,000 in 1999 to under 7,000 today. That decline represents real progress. But the system's underlying logic has not changed. It remains structurally designed to pay for separation rather than stability, to remove rather than resource, and to subject the most vulnerable children to an experience that research increasingly shows makes their long-term trajectories worse, not better.
A quarter of foster children experience three or more placement disruptions — moved from home to home, with each move severing whatever fragile attachments they have managed to form. On average, removed children spend 48% of the observation period out of home. They are not in one stable place. They are cycling through a system that cannot decide what to do with them, at a cost of over $107,000 per child per year, while their families wait — or don't — on the other side.
Visuals:
Cost comparison (typographic layout): "$107,200 to separate. $3,600 to stabilize. The system chose separation." Display as a stark, full-width typographic statement — no chart needed. Place immediately after the Eline story.
Long-term outcomes grid: A set of isolated, large-number callouts — one per screen in scroll-driven format — showing: 28% of homeless population, 20% of prison inmates under 30, 2x teen birth rate, 11–15 percentage point employment reduction. Let each statistic land before the next appears. No surrounding clutter.
Marginal cases bridge callout: A single typographic or highlighted text panel connecting Acts 2 and 3 explicitly: "The cases least likely to be substantiated are the cases where removal causes the most harm." This does not need to be a data visualization — it is a conceptual hinge and should be styled to feel like a verdict.
Placement instability visual: Four simple child icons in a row. One is highlighted or marked differently. Label: "1 in 4 foster children experiences 3 or more placement disruptions." Keep it human-scale and simple — avoid abstract charts here.
Line chart — Foster care population over time: Show the decline from 40,000 (1999) to under 7,000 (2024). Annotate it with the caveat that the decline in volume has not changed the system's logic or cost structure. The cost per child has risen as volume fell.

Act 4: The Alternative That Isn't — CARES
Narrative: In 2024, New York City announced a milestone: 25% of incoming child protection cases are now diverted to CARES — the Collaborative Assessment, Response, Engagement, and Support program — framed as a non-investigatory, family-supportive alternative.
But the case of Dayanara tells a different story. Placed in the CARES non-investigatory track, she had her child removed by an ACS worker who interpreted her speech pattern as evidence of drug use — without a drug test, without court involvement, without law enforcement, and without informing her of her rights. Judicial approval was not sought until six days after the child had already been removed.
CARES is technically voluntary. But if a family is deemed "resistant," the agency can switch the case to a formal investigation. The program operates under the same coercive umbrella as the system it is meant to replace. As The Bronx Defenders document in "This Wound Is Still Fresh": CARES may function as "another surveillance program."
The growth of CARES — from 4% of cases in its early years to 22% in 2024 — looks like reform. But if the pipeline at the SCR remains unreformed, and if CARES retains the capacity for coercive removal without judicial oversight, the label changes while the machine does not.
Visuals:
Sankey diagram — The full pipeline: This is the centerpiece visualization for the whole website. Show the flow from SCR Hotline Calls → Screened Out (25%) / Passed to Agencies (75%) → Routed to CARES / Routed to Investigation → Unsubstantiated / Indicated → No removal / Removal → Foster care. Use color to show racial composition at each stage, demonstrating how the population becomes progressively more Black and Latino as the pipeline narrows toward the most severe interventions. This diagram should be scrollable/interactive — as the reader scrolls, they move through the pipeline.
CARES growth line on the ACS trend chart (from Act 1): Reference back to the line chart showing ACS volume over time, but now add the CARES line growing from 4% to 22%. Label it: "Reform in name. The pipeline remains."

Act 5: The Courthouse — Court-Ordered Supervision
Narrative: For families that reach Family Court, the state formalizes its grip through Article X filings — neglect petitions that overwhelmingly conflate poverty with parental failure. The geography of these filings is not random. It is a map of structural disinvestment.
In FY 2023, Highbridge/Concourse in the Bronx (BX04) saw 1,462 SCR intakes and 149 Article X filings. Park Slope in Brooklyn (BK06) saw 333 intakes and 14 filings. A parent in Park Slope can address a mental health crisis or substance use issue privately, with their own resources. A parent in the South Bronx faces a state-mandated legal battle.
The terror of this process is not abstract. Marianna Azar, recovering from abdominal surgery, refused opioid painkillers — not because of addiction, but because she feared ACS would interpret the medication on her bedside table as evidence of "substance abuse." She spent two nights in excruciating pain, a direct and documented consequence of state-induced fear.
Visuals:
Choropleth map of NYC community districts: Show SCR intake rates and Article X filing rates by neighborhood. Color intensity should reveal the concentration in high-poverty Bronx districts versus lower-poverty areas like Park Slope. Annotate BX04 and BK06 explicitly with their numbers. The map should make the geographic and economic sorting of the system undeniable.
Two-column comparison: BX04 vs. BK06. Stack the numbers side by side — intakes, filings, median household income, poverty rate. No chart needed. The juxtaposition is the argument.

Closing: The System Chooses This
Narrative: This is not a system failing to achieve its goals. It is a system achieving exactly what it was designed to achieve: the surveillance, investigation, and control of Black and Latino families in poverty, under the cover of child protection.
The SCR accepts calls at twice the national average. Investigators enter homes without warrants 99.8% of the time. Black children face investigation at more than twice the rate of white children, and emergency removal at more than double the rate. Families like Eline's lose their children to a system that will not pay $300 to keep them together but will pay $107,200 to keep them apart. CARES, the flagship reform, removes children without court orders and without informing parents of their rights.
The machine runs. And it runs in the Bronx.
What would actually help:
A standardized SCR screening tool, as used by the majority of other states
Judicial oversight before, not six days after, child removal
Resource provision — housing subsidies, visiting nurses, food access — as a first response rather than a last resort
Community-based, non-CPS-led pathways that carry no threat of investigation
Visual: Return to the opening counter. This time, let the reader scroll back through the 95,590 intakes — but now each number carries the weight of everything they've just read. Optionally, end with a single large typographic statement: "23% were ever substantiated. The other 77% were families who had done nothing wrong."
Design Guidance
Take cues from The Pudding: prioritize scroll-driven storytelling where visuals appear and transform as the reader moves through the narrative
Use a restrained, somber palette — not sensationalist. The data is damning enough
Avoid jargon. Every acronym (SCR, ACS, CARES, Article X) should be explained plainly on first use and never assumed
Let real names and real cases (Eline, Dayanara, Marianna, Ebony Gould, Y.A.) anchor the statistics — the human cases should appear as narrative interruptions within the data, not as sidebars
The site should feel like reading a long-form investigative piece, not browsing an infographic dashboard
