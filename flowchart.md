Textual Representation of the Flowchart

1. Overall flow: entry point and first decision

The flowchart starts with a reporting and screening stage. A concern about a child begins with the question “Immediate danger?”. If there is immediate danger, the pathway moves to Call to SCR Hotline and then to Report to ACS. From there, ACS proceeds into a formal Investigation (ACS) process.  ￼

After the report reaches ACS, the next key question is whether “the report meets statutory criteria.” The flowchart lists the legal criteria that must be satisfied:
	•	the child is under 18 and under New York State jurisdiction
	•	the person responsible has a legal duty of care
	•	the minimum degree of care was not displayed
	•	there is imminent risk of impairment  ￼

If the report does not meet these criteria, it does not proceed as a formal maltreatment investigation in the same way. If it does meet the criteria, the process continues into screening and case assignment.  ￼

⸻

2. Report intake and SCR screening

A second entry section in the chart presents the procedural intake in more detail:
	•	Receive Report (ACS or Local CPS)
	•	Assign to CPS worker in 24 hrs
	•	SCR Screening: does the report meet the legal definition for abuse/neglect?  ￼

At this stage, two outcomes are possible:

2.1 If the answer is “No”

The node says “Call is screened out.” This means the report does not proceed into an abuse/neglect investigation.  ￼

2.2 If the answer is “Yes”

The case proceeds to:
	•	Home Visit & Family Engagement
	•	Child Safety Determination  ￼

The chart also says that investigators collect information to identify and locate the child or legally responsible adults, including:
	•	name and address of the child and family members
	•	child’s age, sex, and primary language
	•	nature and extent of injuries
	•	type of abuse or neglect
	•	prior history of abuse or neglect involving the child or siblings
	•	any additional helpful information  ￼

This is the factual intake and case-building part of the process.

⸻

3. Traditional ACS investigation pathway

Once the case is accepted for investigation, the chart shows a formal ACS investigation path. It includes:
	•	Investigation (ACS)
	•	Complete traditional investigation within 60 days
	•	reference to FCA Article 10  ￼

The main investigative components are listed as:
	•	home conditions
	•	food and nutrition
	•	medical neglect
	•	educational neglect
	•	inadequate supervision
	•	substance use by caregiver
	•	domestic violence  ￼

These are the key subject areas ACS examines during the investigation.

⸻

4. Outcomes of the traditional investigation

The node “Investigation Result” branches into multiple possible case outcomes. These include:
	•	Child Removed / Ordered into ACS Care
	•	Court Ordered Supervision
	•	CARES
	•	Case Closed (optional voluntary services)  ￼

The flowchart also attaches names as examples or references beside some outcomes:
	•	Rachel: Placement
	•	Kai: Court Order Supervision
	•	Shraddha: Report & Investigation
	•	Jeffrey: CARES  ￼

These appear to be illustrative labels connected to specific routes in the diagram.

Another branch near the bottom gives formal determination outcomes:
	•	Unfounded
	•	Indicated  ￼

If the matter is Indicated, the chart leads toward court orders, preventive services, and possibly placement-related consequences. If Unfounded, the case may end without substantiation, though referrals can still occur.  ￼

⸻

5. Court-ordered supervision and service plan branch

One branch from the investigation leads to Court Ordered Supervision, and the chart says FSU Specialist Assigned. This implies a formal monitoring and compliance structure after court involvement.  ￼

The chart then shows:
	•	Court Mandated Completion of Service Plan
	•	Failure to meet court order
	•	Successful completion of court order  ￼

This branch represents a compliance pathway. The family is ordered to complete a service plan, and then one of two broad things happens:
	•	failure to comply may prolong or worsen system involvement
	•	successful completion can move toward resolution and closure  ￼

⸻

6. Preventive services branch

Another major part of the flowchart is the preventive services system. The chart includes:
	•	Referral to preventative services
	•	Mandated Preventative services
	•	Preventive Services
	•	Community-Based Services  ￼

6.1 Mandated Preventative Services

The chart lists the following examples:
	•	day care
	•	homemaker services
	•	parent training or parent aide
	•	clinical services
	•	transportation
	•	respite care and services for families when a parent, legal guardian, caretaker, or child has HIV/AIDS or an HIV-related illness
	•	emergency services with 24-hour access
	•	preventive housing services  ￼

6.2 Mandated Only Preventative Services

A separate node names three subcategories:
	•	Intensive, Home-Based Family Preservation Services
	•	Preventive Housing Services
	•	Respite Care and Services for Families  ￼

The flowchart includes detailed explanatory text for each:

Intensive, Home-Based Family Preservation Services
These are direct interventions aimed at restoring or stabilizing family functioning, mainly through teaching and role modeling. They are brief but intensive, initially up to 30 days, with a possible 30-day extension. The caseworker must be available 24/7, and at least half of the services must be delivered in the family’s home.  ￼

Preventive Housing Services
These are used when a family needs financial help to obtain or keep adequate housing so that the child can remain safely cared for. The chart mentions examples such as:
	•	rent subsidies
	•	payment of rent arrears
	•	other assistance necessary to obtain adequate housing  ￼

Respite Care and Services for Families
These are described as brief and temporary care and supervision of children, used when parents are struggling to maintain adequate care because of stress, sudden family deterioration, or unexpected demands. The chart clarifies that “parents” includes biological parents, adoptive parents, stepparents, legal guardians, or caretakers with authority and responsibility for the child.  ￼

⸻

7. Community Optional Services branch

The flowchart also contains a node for Community Optional Services and explains Community Optional Preventive Services (COPS). According to the diagram, COPS are a subset of preventive services intended for families where placement is not immediate or imminent, but where youth and families are at elevated risk of foster care because of community conditions or other circumstances.  ￼

The chart further explains that COPS cases do not require:
	•	individual eligibility determinations
	•	uniform case records for the child and family in the same way required for mandated and non-mandated preventive services cases  ￼

This means the COPS branch is a less formalized preventive support track than the mandated preventive services route.

⸻

8. CARES / FAR pathway

A major alternative route in the diagram is CARES, also identified as FAR.

The chart explicitly states:
	•	Collaborative Assessment, Response, Engagement & Support (CARES) is also known as Family Assessment Response (FAR)  ￼

There are two different CARES/FAR segments in the diagram: one describing eligibility and entry, and one describing how the process works after entry.

8.1 Entry into FAR/CARES

The flowchart shows:
	•	FAR (CARES) Entry – (A)
	•	Receive Report (ACS or Local CPS)
	•	FAR Screening Tool
	•	Start Investigation
	•	Is mandatory investigation
	•	Safety Assessment (24 hours - 7 days)
	•	Immediate Dangers
	•	Safe
	•	Family agree?
	•	if Yes → Enter FAR (CARES)
	•	if No → do not enter FAR and instead continue with investigation  ￼

The chart also says:
	•	SCR classifies report as FAR & keeps record for 10 years
	•	CPS can switch the case back to traditional investigation whenever immediate danger concern arise  ￼

So, CARES/FAR is not purely informal; it still begins from a report, screening, and safety assessment, and it can revert to the traditional investigation track if risks escalate.

8.2 Criteria for CARES

The flowchart lists these criteria:
	1.	No serious child abuse and maltreatment
	2.	No children are in immediate or impending danger of serious harm  ￼

These are the basic threshold conditions for diverting from traditional investigation into a family assessment model.

8.3 Characteristics of CARES during the case

The chart explains that during CARES:
	1.	visits from a CARES CPS are scheduled in advance
	2.	interviews with family contacts are made in partnership with the family
	3.	there is no formal determination of whether child maltreatment occurred
	4.	there is no investigation
	5.	there is no determination of allegations and individual culpability for families reported to the SCR  ￼

This is one of the most important distinctions in the chart: CARES is presented as a collaborative, non-investigative alternative.

8.4 Family-Led Assessment and service planning

After entry into CARES, the chart says:
	•	Family-Led Assessment
	•	look with the family at their strengths, wants, concerns, and needs
	•	support family stabilization
	•	assessment tool: Family-Led Assessment Guide (FLAG)
	•	then create a Service Plan  ￼

This means CARES is structured around collaborative assessment and support planning rather than fault-finding.

8.5 Examples of CARES-related support

The diagram lists examples such as:
	•	parenting sessions
	•	talking about stress or anxiety
	•	making child care plans with neighbors or family
	•	finding after school programs
	•	getting food from church food pantries
	•	and more  ￼

8.6 End points of CARES

The flowchart shows two key end states:
	•	New Safety Concern Arise → the case can shift away from CARES and back into investigation
	•	Case Close after 60 Days → End  ￼

So the CARES route is designed as a time-limited assessment and support process with a closure point after 60 days if no new safety concerns appear.

9. Placement and foster care pathway

If the investigation or court process results in removal, the chart maps a placement decision structure.

The sequence begins with:
	•	Order of Placement
	•	Nicholas Scoppetta Children’s Center (NSCC)  ￼

Then it asks a series of placement decision questions.

9.1 Kinship availability

First question:
	•	Kinship Available?

If Yes:
	•	Kinship Foster Care (Preferred Placement)  ￼

If No, the chart continues to assess the child’s needs.

9.2 Complex medical needs

Next question:
	•	Complex Medical Needs?

If Yes:
	•	Specialized / Medical Foster Care  ￼

If No, the chart goes to behavioral/trauma assessment.

9.3 Significant trauma or behavioral needs

Next question:
	•	Significant Trauma / Behavioral Needs?

If Yes:
	•	Enhanced Family Foster Care (EFFC)  ￼

If No, the chart asks about family-setting viability.

9.4 Family setting viability

Question:
	•	Family Setting Viability?

If Yes:
	•	Traditional Foster Care (Private Residence)

If No:
	•	Residential Placement (Group Home)  ￼

This portion of the chart therefore shows a progressive placement logic:
	1.	kinship first,
	2.	then medical needs,
	3.	then trauma/behavioral needs,
	4.	then whether a family setting is viable,
	5.	and only after that, residential/group placement.  ￼

11. End states across the whole flowchart

Across the entire chart, the main end states can be summarized as follows:
	•	the report is screened out
	•	the investigation is completed as unfounded
	•	the investigation is indicated
	•	the family is referred to preventive services
	•	the family enters CARES/FAR
	•	the child is placed under court-ordered supervision
	•	the child is removed and placed into ACS care
	•	the child is routed into a specific foster care or residential setting
	•	the case is closed, with or without voluntary services  ￼
